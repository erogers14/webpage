A Decline in Dropout Rates

Over the past decades, the United States has launched many initiatives to decrease high school dropout rates. One of these initiatives, the White House’s Building a Grad Nation Alliance1, aims to increase graduation rates to 90% by 20202. Reports from this initiative have shown improvement to increase graduation rates, however dropout rates haven’t decreased at a rate higher than 1% since 1960.

The data3 we used was from the Current Population Survey (CPS)4 run by the U.S. Census Bureau. From the interactive line graph5 above, we can learn that high school dropout rates in the United States have a downward trend as year increases. All years after 1966 had a dropout rate below 17%.

We set off to investigate dropout trends across the 50 states in the United States. Our goal was to analyze a midpoint mark, 2010, in a more in depth analysis. We were interested to see whether this trend was true across races and socioeconomic status, since the common narrative does not often highlight vulnerable and minority populations as their main focus.

The United States has been lagging behind on graduation rates among the most developed countries6. We wanted to take into consideration these rates among states, since some states in the United States are comparable in size and population to certain developed nations. Besides analyzing trends across states, we also wanted to consider two factors that researchers claim affect dropout rates the most: poverty and income level7. We did so by considering the median household income and poverty level of each state. Click on our “More Info” tab to learn about our findings.
